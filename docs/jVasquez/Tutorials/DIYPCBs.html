Notes on Double-Sided PCB Etching

Halfway there to halfway-decent PCBs

For me, PCB etching is still very much an experimental craft. Getting good results tends to take several tries. Luckily, I've ironed out (quite literally) most of the techniques that don't work as easily as stated.
What follows is an honest collection of notes for DIY double-sided PCB ecthing. These notes help eliminate most of the easy pitfalls in the etching process. According to notes and forums online, I'm using the toner-transfer method with cupric chloride.

Materials

Often replacing the right tool with the wrong tool doesn't just yield a working, but less-admirable result; it usually doesn't result in anything. Using the right tools has been key for getting a good PCB.

Final Comments

One last note: After some thorough hand-testing, I have yet to find myself needing to debounce the encoder inputs. I will implement this feature for safety after double-checking how much deboucing I need to perform to ensure that the encoder never samples within the rise or fall time of the waveform of either channel.

As a brief note: the average rise and fall times from the datasheet are 100 and 150 nanoseconds. a 50 [MHz] clock will drive the state machine to sample the waveforms every 20 nanoseconds. To effectively debounce, I should take multiple samples of the waveform, ensuring that they fall out of the rise/fall time of the waveforms. From there, I suggest rewriting the module to output a state change only when all samples are in agreement of the waveform value detected.
