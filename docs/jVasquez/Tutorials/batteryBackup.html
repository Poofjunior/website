Diode Tricks I: Backup Battery Power

Adding a secondary power supply to the same circuit

Losing power for just a moment can sometimes be an issue. Perhaps unlogged data could be lost forever, or perhaps the chip keeping time could reset itself --and the time with it! Here's a quick circuit that let's you connect two power supplies to the same circuit using diodes. In the sections below I'll build on the same circuit to add a feature that also charges the battery while the main power is connected.

The Basic Setup

The circuit above depicts two power supplies connected to the same circuit. Keep in mind that both diodes are assumed to have the same properties, (i.e: the same forward voltage, Vf). Here, the higher voltage of the two powers the circuit while the other source does nothing. When The power supply kicks out, the backup battery takes over. In the circuit receiving the input voltage, bypass capacitors must be present to ensure that the transition between voltage sources doesn't interfere with the circuit's operation.

Extra Features

It's also possible to charge the battery (if rechargeable), while the input power supply is on. The circuit below depicts such a circuit:

Note that the circuit will not charge a multi-cell Lithium Polymer (LiPo) battery! Additional circuitry must be added to ensure that each individual cell in the pack is kept at the same level so that the entire pack is balanced. Of course, adding in even a single cell LiPo battery introduces complexity. Namely, we, the designers, must remember that the cell should not be discharged below 3.0 [V].

Hardware Implementation

To test this circuit, I connected a 6 [V] power supply (some AAs) as the main power supply with a single cell LiPo as the backup battery. The main circuit is just an LED and a resistor. For the backup battery, don't forget that a LiPo cell is "fully charged" when the cell voltage reaches 4.2 [V]. Thus, the zener diode should have a breakdown voltage of about 4.2 [V] as well. (I didn't actually have a zener diode handy, so I strung together a collection of 1N4001 diodes until they produced a forward voltage of about 4.2 [V].) Lastly, the resistor that lets current flow to charge the backup battery is 470 [Ohms].

Final Notes

In a real circuit I'd definitely add those bypass capacitors to the main circuit. All-in-all, a circuit like this one should keep that wall clock ticking for just a bit longer before the power comes back on.
