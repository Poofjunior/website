Underwater Thrust Bearings

Introduction

Facilitating electrically-driven movement underwater can be tricky. Above water, motors are the fairly straightforward solution to generate movement. Underwater, motors can provide the same solution, but their electrical components must be sufficiently insulated--or entirely isolated--from the water to prevent short-circuits. This small mechanical design is a proof-of-concept for facilitating underwater rotation with an electric motor while isolating the motor from the water. The device works well for low-torque applications.

Design Overview

The bearing is made of several thin layers shown in the cutaway image above. Three thin (.025") delrin sheets house approximately 180 1/16th" plastic bearing balls with about 90 balls per side. Each layer of bearing balls consist of two concentric rings of bearings. In the image below, the upper layer has been removed as ball bearings are loaded into their concentric cavities. To create the cavities, I simply laser-cut a smaller disc and ring of delrin.



The upper layer is the surface that the underwater application uses, whether its a motor, shaft, or some other rotating device. The lower surface is driven by the motor. Two sets of neodymium magnets (not shown above) couple motion from the bottom to the top side of the bearing. Given that The plastic bearing balls and delrin sheets form a gap between the two sets of magnets of .2", both concentric sets of magnets can attract each other with enough force to translate low-torque movement between both sides.

Conclusion

The entire setup can be reproduced with just a laser cutter, the only precision instrument needed to cut the delrin discs. Overall, this design demonstrates an inexpensive method of creating custom thrust bearings that will withstand abuse in an underwater environment.
