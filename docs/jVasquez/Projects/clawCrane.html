The Claw Crane

the E155 MicroPs final project with Nicole Yu

Perhaps you too can remember them? The claw-cranes that teased us with the promise of a stuffed animal and ate your change ate your change in response? We weren't looking to relive those days of disappointment; thus, we created the winnable claw crane.

With a bit more context, E155: Microprocessors (aka: MicroPs) is Harvey Mudd's upper-division introduction to embedded systems. It's the bread-and-butter class for the digital designer and a fresh look beyond what the world of Arduino can offer. This partnered final project boasts the integration of both an FPGA and a microprocessor in a common system.


Physical Design

This project wasn't just an electronics endeavor but also a head-first plunge into gantries. After a few visits to the machine shop, we had constructed a crude (but functional!) two-axis gantry that would demonstrate the functionality of the electronics.

The acrylic blocks hold both the press-fit bearings and the two hardened rods. To achieve the press-fit tolerances on the holes, we predrilled each hole with the closest undersize drill bit and then finished the hole with a boring tool.

The center box is constructed from eighth-inch acetal delrin cut with the laser cutter. Like many other projects fabricated with the laser cutter, this box is joined with puzzle-piece-style edges. Since delrin is fairly non-reactive with most glues and epoxies, we press fit each of the plates together. Offsetting the laser such that outer edges are larger by 0.003" resulted in a fairly tight press fit.

Electronics

The electronics are fairly straightforward. A finite-state machine implemented on the FPGA governs the overall behavior of the four motors (two for the gantry and two for the claw crane). The PIC's sole responsibility is to monitor when the claw has closed in on the prize and the motor motion has jammed. To accomplish this, an analog hall-effect sensor attached to the shaft stops spinning when the claw can no longer close. The PIC sends a signal to the FPGA, which ceases to drive the claw closed.
