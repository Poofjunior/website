Introduction

This summer, I took some time to learn the ins-and-outs of programming the CNC mill. As a machining tool, it's an incredible upgrade from the manual milling machines. Rather than having to manually move the mill to a particular location, it can be programmed to perform that operation automatically. Furthermore, the milling machine can produce arcs and pockets, a task well-outside the scope of the manual mills. With the CNC mill, many projects impossible with the manual mills are now realizable with a bit of time and some programming effort.

The Motivation

At the same time, I've become fond of creating props with the paper modeling art style. paper modeling involves decomposing a 3D model into a separated 2D mesh, printing the pattern, and then folding it up into a real-life duplicate of the model. Of course, this process requires cutting out the printed pattern by hand, generally one of the less-exciting steps.

The solution? It turns out that the HMC laser cutter cuts through paper like butter! (... er, as we all might've expected.) By exporting the pattern to a .dxf file, one can skip the tediousness of cutting out the pattern and have a machine perform this operation more gracefully (and far more precisely) than most of our scissor-equipped fingers.

Of course, I'll be departing from Mudd soon, so the only natural permanent solution is to build my own paper laser-cutting tool. With a bit more time and effort, countless hours can be saved from hand-cutting with a glorious laser cutter to do the job automatically.

Mechanical Design and Components

Two-axis gantries aren't new; in fact, companies sell entire "linear motion systems" tailored to your particular application. For the "starving student," though, I'll just have to settle with the satisfaction of building my own.

The time-saver: 80/20 Extruded Aluminum Beams

Luckily, 80/20, the "erector set of the engineer," isn't too expensive. (The brackets and myriad of connectors are a different story though.) This is my first time exploring it as an option. Furthermore, McMaster-Carr provides 3D Solidworks models of 80/20 and several brackets, a massive time-saving when I started developing a 3d model.

The H-bot

To move the laser platform along two axes, I've opted for an H-bot design. H-bots provide the benefit of two-axis motion with a lighter moving platform as opposed to other gantries. The reason for the weight-loss? Both motors are stationary and neither is located on any of the moving platforms. In many extrusive 3D printers, one motor is typically located on one of the moving axes to move the extruder head.

The Build Log

I'll admit that the design took a few months CADing designs over and over again until I had a build that was machinable, relatively inexpensive, and easily to assemble. For the precision motion, I had discovered a pile of hardened rods and bushings on ebay, so I picked those up for far less than I would pay from most online retailers. I worked with these rods and bushings as a design constraint, about which the rest of the gantry was formed.

As I soon realized, a number of other components were far out of my budget. I also hoped not to spend too long in the machine shop making custom versions of my own parts that were difficult to reproduce. To work around these constraints, I opted to design parts that didn't require a professional skill level to fabricate.

DIY Pulleys


With ease-of-reproducability in mind, I discovered a quick way to make DIY pulleys that simply involved sandwiching two delrin discs over a skate bearing. I did machine the shafts, but all of the other components either already existed or were quickly reproduceable with the laser cutter. The net result works surprisingly well.

Adding Belt Tension
