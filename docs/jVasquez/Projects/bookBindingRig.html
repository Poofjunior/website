Book-Binding Rig

Introduction

Before an EReader found its way into my backpack, I'd generally find myself printing each article to read for research paper assignments. Even now, let's face it, no annotation software has been able to trump the freedom of pen on paper. Unfortunately, I quickly realized that these research papers were far too thick to staple together conveniently; some papers were just over 60 pages long! The solution: a book-binding rig to turn even the thickest research article into a humble paperback.



Now, I can simply print the pages, insert them into the binder, run a thin film of wood along the spine, and head off to class. When I return a few hours later, a freshly-pressed coverless book awaits me. I'm impressed just how well these wood-glue spines hold actually hold together; I can even crease pages open without damaging the spine.



In retrospect, printing 60-page documents is a bit wasteful, so I've curbed my book-binding for now. Of course, when writing a research paper, I still find that my own physical copy of the evidence is much more convenient to flip through over any e-copy, so the book-binding rig does get its occasional use in that context.
